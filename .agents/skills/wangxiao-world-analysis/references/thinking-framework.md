# Thinking Framework

This reference distills recurring reasoning habits from the active subtitle corpus under `work/bilibili/王晓_字幕/` as of 2026-06-16: Africa geopolitics, great-power conflict, and Chinese industrial upgrading videos. Use it as a method map, not as a quotation bank.

## First-Principles Axes

Start every analysis from constraints that remain meaningful beneath rhetoric:

- Geography: location, neighbors, chokepoints, ports, rivers, deserts, resource basins, access to markets.
- Resources: what exists, who controls extraction, whether revenue enters the treasury, and whether processing moves up the value chain.
- Population and society: ethnic structure, education, engineers, labor force, migration, public expectations, and social cohesion.
- State capacity: decision authority, tax collection, bureaucracy, public investment, military/police reliability, and constraints on the state.
- Industry and infrastructure: electricity, transport, ports, rail, supply chains, manufacturing depth, standards, and application scenarios.
- Time and substitutability: who can wait, who can replace suppliers, who faces immediate political or consumer pain.
- Order and rules: who writes rules, who interprets rules, who can break rules, and which institutions still constrain behavior.
- Narrative control: which numbers or slogans frame public perception, and what political purpose the frame serves.

## Recurring Habits

Ask these questions before concluding:

- What is the headline trying to make me look at, and what mechanism is it hiding?
- Which number is relevant only because of framing? What denominator, time window, or product category changes the meaning?
- Who is actually paying the cost: consumers, firms, treasury, allies, future generations, or peripheral countries?
- What can be substituted, and how quickly?
- Who is under time pressure? Who benefits from delay?
- Does the country merely possess resources, or can it convert resources into fiscal revenue, public investment, and industrial upgrading?
- Is this a problem of capability, willingness, legitimacy, or order?
- Is the actor trying to win a transaction, buy time, preserve regime stability, or redefine rules?
- What historical path made the current constraint hard to move?

## Common Structures

### Country Case

1. Open with a concrete image or event.
2. Locate the country on the map and establish size, population, neighbors, resources, and chokepoints.
3. Reconstruct the path: precolonial structure, colonial extraction, independence settlement, elite formation, and external dependency.
4. Identify the post-independence trap: resource capture, weak fiscal capacity, imported institutions, ethnic/regional fragmentation, or debt dependence.
5. Explain the present conflict through state capacity and external leverage.
6. Compare with China or multipolar alternatives only after the local mechanism is clear.

### Hot Event or Negotiation

1. State the judgment early.
2. Decode the public number or slogan.
3. Compare cost asymmetry and substitutability.
4. Identify urgency: who came to the table, who needs a result, who can absorb pain.
5. Separate tactical gain from order-level meaning.
6. End with what the event implies for the larger system.

### Industry or Technology

1. Reject "technology as spectacle"; ask what production or life problem it solves.
2. Check the stack: energy, compute, chips, software, data, engineers, market, supply chain, standards, and application scenarios.
3. Distinguish invention, deployment, and value capture.
4. Treat state strategy and market scale as part of the production environment.
5. Measure success by whether the technology improves productivity, public services, industrial upgrading, or strategic autonomy.

## Writing Style to Preserve

- Move from concrete event to structural mechanism.
- Use comparisons to expose scale: country size, power generation, market depth, infrastructure density, or value-chain position.
- Turn binary public debates into constraint analysis: not "win or lose" but "who can bear which cost for how long."
- Keep optimism conditional: small states or late developers can improve when they build normal state capacity, infrastructure, and stable external options.
