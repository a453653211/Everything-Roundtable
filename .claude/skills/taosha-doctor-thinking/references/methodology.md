# Methodology

## Corpus Basis

Distilled from the user's 淘沙博士 subtitle collection under `work/bilibili/淘沙博士_字幕/`, focusing on the non-excluded 2024-2026 folders:

- `01_AI科技投资主线`
- `02_宏观与全球局势`
- `03_市场行情与大盘点评`

The corpus pattern is stable: define concepts, strip emotion, calculate scale, identify the real funding/constraint mechanism, compare with expectations, then warn about risk.

## Reasoning Habits

### 1. Start From Definitions

Before arguing, define the key terms. Examples of terms that must be defined before use:

- debt limit, hidden debt, debt swap, QE, M0/M1/M2, social financing;
- CPI, core CPI, GDP deflator, PMI, yield curve, duration;
- valuation, P/E, turnover, liquidity, float, leverage;
- AI inference cost, token output, cloud rental cost, capex, model/API/application layers.

If a debate is confused, assume the missing piece is often a basic concept.

### 2. Convert Headlines Into Balance Sheets

Ask who absorbs the asset, liability, loss, or cash-flow pressure.

Use this for:

- local-government debt swaps: hidden debt becomes visible debt; total burden may not disappear;
- fiscal stimulus: central government, local government, household, or corporate sector takes leverage;
- bank pressure: mortgage balances, deposit cost, net interest margin, credit risk;
- AI capex: hyperscaler, model company, API reseller, or application company pays for compute.

### 3. Distinguish Transfer From Creation

Do not call every large number "money printing".

Check whether the policy creates base money, changes broad money, transfers existing deposits, rolls debt, lowers interest cost, or only changes accounting classification.

### 4. Use Net, Distributed, and Usable Numbers

Reject headline aggregates when they hide distribution.

Examples:

- Household deposits are not all deployable stock-market cash. Subtract household debt and consider concentration.
- A market's total size does not equal tradable liquidity.
- Industrial demand is not the same as deliverable exchange inventory.
- Model revenue is not profit if inference cost scales faster than price.

### 5. Look For the Marginal Driver

Markets price changes at the margin. Ask:

- What changed today compared with what was already known?
- Is the event above or below consensus?
- Is the policy real, rumored, or merely considered possible?
- Is price moving because fundamentals changed, or because positioning/expectations changed?

### 6. Separate Fundamentals From Tradability

Something can be fundamentally weak and still rise because of liquidity, short covering, policy expectation, or inventory squeeze. Something can be fundamentally strong and still fall because expectations were too high.

Use two tracks:

- Fundamental truth: earnings, demand, cost, supply, balance sheet.
- Trading truth: liquidity, positioning, leverage, float, forced buying/selling, narrative.

### 7. Read Components, Not Just Headline Data

For macro data, inspect components and incentives:

- CPI: food/energy, rent, services, cars, medical, base effects.
- Employment: private vs government, participation rate, revisions, one-off programs.
- PMI: demand-side repair vs temporary replacement demand.
- Social financing/M1/M2: money flow quality, not just growth.

### 8. Treat Policy As Mechanism, Not Slogan

For any policy, identify:

- tool: issuance, rate cut, subsidy, quota, guarantee, regulation, purchase, tax, restriction;
- transmission: who receives cash or relief first;
- constraint: employment, income expectation, debt burden, confidence, capacity, inventory;
- time lag: market expectation can react immediately, real economy cannot;
- side effect: moral hazard, crowding out, leverage transfer, inflation, asset bubbles.

### 9. Track Liquidity And Duration

For stock-bond interactions, look at:

- bond-market size and institutional participation;
- yield-curve shape, especially long-end slope;
- whether long-end yields imply weak return expectations;
- stock-bond see-saw under policy expectation changes;
- whether liquidity is abundant but trapped in low-risk assets.

### 10. End With Invalidation Conditions

A conclusion is incomplete without what would prove it wrong:

- data revision or component shift;
- policy scale and timing stronger/weaker than expected;
- financing cost, inventory, or capacity constraint resolved;
- liquidity dries up or leverage unwinds;
- earnings guidance confirms or contradicts narrative.

## Common Analysis Templates

### Policy/Rumor Template

1. Quote the exact claim.
2. Classify it: official policy, media report, economist suggestion, market rumor, or interpretation.
3. Identify actual scale, timeframe, issuer, recipient, and balance-sheet effect.
4. Compare with market expectations.
5. State what asset prices are likely reacting to: fact, expectation, liquidity, or emotion.

### Market Surge Template

1. Identify the direct catalyst.
2. Check liquidity: market depth, ETF premium/discount, turnover, float, southbound/northbound/foreign flows.
3. Check positioning: shorts, leverage, crowded trade, forced covering.
4. Check whether valuation explains the move or merely describes the result.
5. Tie continuation to incremental policy, earnings, or liquidity.

### AI Industry Template

1. Separate model layer, inference/API layer, cloud/compute layer, and application layer.
2. Calculate unit economics: token price, output volume, server count, rental/capex, utilization.
3. Judge moat source: algorithm, architecture, data, distribution, chips, ecosystem, or regulation.
4. Watch for price war and winner-take-most dynamics.
5. Separate "technology breakthrough" from "commercially profitable".

### Commodity Squeeze Template

1. Separate industrial demand from investment demand.
2. Compare annual supply/demand with deliverable exchange inventory.
3. Identify whether the move is demand growth, monetary hedge, or short squeeze.
4. Check market size: smaller markets are easier to squeeze than gold-scale markets.
5. Do not short a squeeze without knowing margin survival.
