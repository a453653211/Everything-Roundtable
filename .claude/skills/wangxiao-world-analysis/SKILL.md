# Wangxiao World Analysis

## Overview

Use this skill to reason about international politics, country cases, industrial competition, and macro events in the style distilled from Wang Xiao/Albert subtitle material. Treat it as an analytical lens, not as a fact database.

## Core Workflow

1. Frame the concrete question, event, actors, and time window. Avoid answering the headline; answer the underlying strategic problem.
2. Separate surface narrative from structural constraint: visible numbers, slogans, and announcements are symptoms; costs, substitutions, time pressure, production capacity, fiscal capacity, and rule control are mechanisms.
3. Build a first-principles map using the axes in `references/thinking-framework.md`: geography, resources, population, state capacity, industry, infrastructure, security, external dependence, rules, and historical path.
4. Identify each actor's binding constraint: who lacks time, who lacks substitutes, who pays the direct cost, who needs legitimacy, who controls rules, and who can convert resources into public investment.
5. Check sources with the route in `references/source-map.md`. Prioritize maps, official primary documents, international institutions, industry data, academic work, and media narratives according to the claim type.
6. Produce a compact answer: one-line judgment, surface-vs-structure table, actor incentives, decisive constraints, evidence used or still needed, uncertainty, and likely next moves.

## Output Pattern

For most user requests, structure the answer as:

- `Judgment`: the main conclusion in one sentence.
- `Surface`: what the public debate is focusing on.
- `Structure`: the deeper mechanism that decides the outcome.
- `Actors`: incentives, constraints, and leverage.
- `Evidence`: sources checked or source categories still required.
- `Implication`: what changes if this analysis is right.

## Reference Routing

- Read `references/thinking-framework.md` when the task asks for Wang Xiao-style reasoning, article structure, first-principles analysis, or how to think through a new case.
- Read `references/source-map.md` when the task asks where to collect data, how to verify claims, or what source stack to use.
- Read `references/experience-notes.md` when the task asks for durable macro conclusions, country/industry/conflict research templates, or bottom-level lessons from the subtitle corpus.

## Guardrails

- Do not imitate catchphrases, insults, or rhetorical persona. Preserve the analytical habits, not the performative style.
- Do not treat old subtitle claims as current facts. For live politics, markets, laws, leaders, wars, or company/industry data, verify with current sources.
- Distinguish evidence from inference. Label "Wang-style inference" when extrapolating from structural logic rather than a directly sourced fact.
- Avoid one-factor explanations. Most cases require at least two layers: material constraints plus institutional or order-level constraints.
