# Source Map

The subtitle corpus repeatedly points to a practical source stack. Use source categories according to claim type; do not rely on a single prestige source.

## Common Source Categories

| Claim type | Preferred sources | Use pattern |
|---|---|---|
| Geography and chokepoints | Maps, satellite imagery, port/rail/pipeline maps, official geography pages | Start with map reality before ideology. |
| Basic country indicators | UN, World Bank, IMF, OECD, national statistics, central banks | Establish population, GDP, debt, trade, electricity, poverty, and fiscal capacity. |
| International rules | UN resolutions, WTO documents, treaty texts, sanctions lists, court/arbitration records | Distinguish formal rule from enforcement power. |
| Official intent | Government white papers, party congress reports, ministry statements, customs/tariff notices, development plans | Use to infer stated strategy and policy sequence. |
| Industry structure | Industry associations, customs data, electricity data, company filings, market research, supply-chain reports | Check scale, substitutability, bottlenecks, and value capture. |
| Academic background | Papers, books, theses, historical monographs, area studies | Use for colonial history, ethnic structure, institutions, and long path dependence. |
| Think-tank framing | RAND and other strategy think tanks, policy institutes, university centers | Treat as elite agenda and policy option signal, not neutral truth. |
| Media narrative | NYT, FT, BBC, Reuters, WSJ, local media, Chinese outlets such as Guancha | Use to compare narrative frames, leaks, and elite messaging. Cross-check factual claims. |
| Field/guest material | Interviews, travel observation, specialist guests, industry practitioners | Use for texture and hypotheses; verify core facts separately. |

## Collection Workflow

1. Map first: identify terrain, neighbors, logistics, resource location, and route dependence.
2. Build a data sheet: population, fiscal revenue, debt, exports/imports, electricity, infrastructure, education, industrial base, and military/security basics.
3. Add historical path: colonial ruler, administrative borders, elite education, independence settlement, civil war/coup record, resource concessions, and external debt.
4. Add current actors: government, opposition, military, business groups, foreign powers, institutions, and companies.
5. Cross-check narratives: compare official statements, Western media framing, local media, and international institutions.
6. Only then write the structural judgment.

## Reliability Notes

- International institutions are useful for standardized indicators, but their policy prescriptions and lending incentives require scrutiny.
- Western mainstream media is valuable for elite signaling and leaks, but the framing often reflects its own geopolitical position.
- Official Chinese policy documents are strong for stated strategic direction and sequencing; do not use them alone for outcome claims.
- Market reports and industry data often disagree because definitions differ. Always check category boundaries, date ranges, and whether units are shipments, sales, revenue, installed base, or capacity.
- For fast-moving events, current verification overrides all subtitle-derived background.
