# Experience Notes

These are reusable conclusions distilled from the corpus. Treat them as hypotheses and analysis shortcuts, not immutable laws.

## Macro And Policy

- Debt does not disappear; it moves across balance sheets, maturities, interest rates, and visibility. A debt swap may reduce interest pressure and improve supervision without creating new broad stimulus.
- "Large number" does not equal "money printing". Check whether base money expands, whether M2 changes, and whether existing money is merely reallocated.
- When the economy is demand-constrained, supply-side capacity alone cannot solve weak consumption. Durable recovery requires employment, income expectations, and distribution channels.
- Government leverage can replace private-sector deleveraging, but the key question is whether it creates future cash flow or only shifts the burden.
- Policy expectations can reprice assets quickly; real-economy effects arrive with lag and may be weaker than the initial market move.

## Stock-Bond-Macro Linkage

- Bond markets matter because they price economy-wide return expectations and financing costs. Equity investors who ignore bonds miss the discount-rate and confidence channel.
- A flat or distorted long-end yield curve can imply weak long-term return expectations and liquidity preference. Cutting short rates alone may not fix confidence.
- In a liquidity-rich but confidence-weak environment, stocks and bonds can trade like a see-saw around policy expectations: stronger fiscal impulse may favor equities and pressure bonds; disappointment may do the reverse.
- Valuation is often a result, not a cause. A market can remain cheap until expectations change.

## Market Microstructure

- Thin liquidity can exaggerate moves. ETF premiums, low-float stocks, small commodity markets, and crowded shorts can move far beyond what fundamentals alone justify.
- Always separate liquidity-driven speculation from demand-driven investment logic.
- In squeeze markets, the decisive variable is often deliverable inventory versus leveraged claims, not annual production alone.
- Do not fight a trend solely because it looks irrational. Identify the unwind trigger and whether margin can survive until then.

## Data Skepticism

- Headline data can be technically correct and analytically misleading. Inspect components, revisions, samples, and political or market incentives.
- Employment data requires structure: private versus government jobs, participation rate, revisions, temporary programs, and relation to ADP or other corroborating data.
- CPI requires structure: rent, services, energy, vehicles, medical, and base effects can tell different stories from the headline.
- Market reaction may ignore data quality if liquidity and narrative dominate. Distinguish "true" from "tradable".

## AI And Technology

- Compute brute force has diminishing returns when architecture or algorithm is the real bottleneck. Watch model efficiency, inference cost, and open-source diffusion, not only GPU count.
- AI value chains have different economics at each layer: model creator, cloud/API reseller, compute lessor, application vendor, and end customer.
- API/inference businesses can lose more as usage grows when token prices are below compute cost. Unit economics beat user growth narratives.
- Price wars and open-source breakthroughs can destroy margins for middle layers while expanding adoption.
- Technology breakthrough is not the same as investable profit. Ask who captures value after commoditization.

## Geopolitics And Resources

- Reserve assets and strategic commodities often react to trust, sanctions, war risk, and currency confidence before ordinary supply-demand models catch up.
- Gold's long-term logic in the corpus is monetary/credit hedging; shorter-term moves often come from uncertainty, real-rate expectations, and geopolitical risk.
- For silver/platinum/palladium-style moves, distinguish monetary hedging, industrial use, investment demand, and exchange inventory squeezes.
